# SaaS Marketing

Use this for SaaS landing pages, pricing pages, feature pages, product marketing pages, and signup-entry experiences.

## Marketing Goal

SaaS marketing UI should help a specific audience understand the offer, trust it, and take the next step. It can be more expressive than product UI, but expression should serve positioning, clarity, and conversion.

Before coding, answer:

- Who is the page for?
- What problem, outcome, or category is being offered?
- What is the primary conversion action?
- What proof does the visitor need before acting?
- What visual tone fits the product and audience?

## Page Structure

- Start with a first viewport that makes the product, category, or offer immediately clear.
- Make the primary CTA obvious and repeat it at natural decision points.
- Support the headline with concrete value, not vague adjectives.
- Use sections that answer a buyer's sequence of questions: what it is, who it is for, why it matters, how it works, proof, pricing or next step.
- Keep navigation simple and oriented around conversion or evaluation.
- Let a hint of the next section appear below the hero when possible so the page feels scrollable and grounded.

## Hero Guidance

- The H1 should usually be the product name, offer, category, or a concrete outcome.
- Supporting copy should explain the value proposition and audience fit.
- Avoid generic split hero layouts when a stronger product visual, screenshot, workflow preview, or immersive background would explain the offer better.
- Do not put hero text in a decorative card unless the brand system already depends on that pattern.
- Use product-relevant visuals: screenshots, UI previews, dashboards, workflow scenes, diagrams, or generated assets that clarify the actual offer.

## Conversion and Trust

- Use one primary CTA label consistently.
- Add secondary CTAs only when they serve a real evaluation path, such as viewing docs, watching a demo, or exploring pricing.
- Place proof near claims: customer names, numbers, testimonials, security notes, integrations, or concrete examples.
- Make pricing, trial, demo, or signup expectations clear when relevant.
- Reduce friction around the CTA: avoid asking visitors to interpret too many competing actions.

## Visual Direction

- Marketing pages may use stronger art direction than product UI: richer imagery, more expressive layout, larger type, deeper contrast, or brand-led color.
- Keep the direction coherent across sections. Do not mix unrelated illustration styles, color systems, icon treatments, or card styles.
- Avoid generic SaaS template filler: meaningless badges, vague feature grids, fake dashboard cards, abstract gradient blobs, and social proof without context.
- Prefer fewer, more convincing sections over many shallow sections.

## Relationship to Product UI

Marketing does not need to share the exact product UI system, but it should feel related to the product. Use brand colors, typography, screenshots, component motifs, or interaction language to create continuity without forcing admin-screen density onto a landing page.
